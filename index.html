Between the Linez

A story about chance, courage, and the space between hello and forever.

Some of the greatest love stories begin with the smallest moments — a glance across a room, a borrowed umbrella, a missed train. This is yours to write.

Chapter 1
The Coffee Shop ☕

The Stranger in the Corner

It's a grey Tuesday in October. Raindrops race each other down the glass of Parchment & Brew, the little café on Aldgate Street where you come every morning to feel like yourself before the world asks you to be someone else.

You've just ordered your usual — oat flat white, no sugar — when you notice them.

Sitting at the corner table with a battered copy of One Hundred Years of Solitude, hair still damp from the rain, completely unaware of how effortlessly they've stolen all the light in the room.

Your order is called. The barista slides your cup across the counter. And then — impossibly — the stranger looks up and meets your eyes.

Chapter 2
The Bold Move 🌹

"Is This Seat Taken?"

Your heart does something ridiculous. You walk over anyway.

"Is this seat taken?" you ask, gesturing to the empty chair across from them.

They look up from their book — and you see it now: eyes the colour of old amber, warm and surprised and a little amused.

"It is now," they say, and close García Márquez like they've been waiting for an interruption just like you.

Their name is Jordan. They're an architect who reads magical realism to remind themselves that buildings don't have to be boring. They have a laugh that starts slowly and then suddenly fills the whole room.

Forty minutes vanish. Your coffee goes cold. You don't notice.

Then Jordan's phone buzzes. They glance at it and something shifts behind their eyes — brief, like a cloud passing.

Chapter 3
Into the Rain 🌧️

The Phone Call That Changed Things

Jordan looks at you for a moment — measuring, deciding — and then says something you don't expect.

"My sister is in hospital. She's fine, but... I was supposed to visit her last week and I kept postponing it. I do that. I postpone things."

There's something unbearably honest about it. You reach across the table — not quite touching — and say: "Then go. I'll still be here tomorrow. Same table."

Jordan stands, wraps a scarf around their neck. At the door, they turn back.

"Same table. Seven thirty?"

You nod. And they walk out into the rain like someone who just made a decision about more than one thing.

The next morning, you're at the café at 7:15. Your palms are sweating around your cup. At 7:29, the bell above the door rings.

Chapter 3
The Number 🗒️

A Name on a Napkin

You give them an easy out. "Sounds like you need to go. It was really nice talking to you."

Jordan smiles — a slow, grateful smile — and scribbles a number on the corner of their book receipt, tearing it off and sliding it across the table.

"I'm not usually this easy to talk to," they say. "That means something. Text me?"

You sit alone for a while after they leave, turning the receipt over in your fingers. It smells faintly of rain and coffee.

That night you stare at your phone. The receipt is on your nightstand.

Chapter 4
First Messages 💬

The Reply

Your phone lights up eleven minutes later.

"She always finishes. That's the tragedy of her."

Then: "Also hi. I was hoping you'd text."

You spend the next two weeks sending each other sentences from books you love. Then voice notes. Then a very long phone call that ends at 2am because neither of you will be the first to hang up.

You meet again — not at the café, but at a bookshop closing sale where you both independently panic-bought the same obscure French novella. Jordan holds up two copies and laughs that room-filling laugh and something inside you quietly rearranges itself into a new shape.

Chapter 4
Three Days ⏳

Better Late

Three days, fourteen hours, and twenty-two minutes after Jordan handed you that receipt, you type: "Hi. Sorry for the delay. I'm someone who overthinks things."

The reply comes in four seconds.

"Hi. I'm someone who over-feels things. I think we're going to be a disaster together."

"Or the opposite," you type.

"Yes," they write back. "Exactly the opposite."

The first date lasts seven hours. You walk the whole city. Jordan points at buildings and tells you secrets about their bones — how the old post office downtown was built to hold the weight of a thousand love letters. That's why it's still standing, they say.

Chapter 2
The Patient Heart 🍂

Same Café, Different Mornings

You look away. Find your usual table by the window. Open your laptop. Tell yourself it's fine.

But something makes you come back the next morning. And the morning after that.

They're there both days — same corner, different books. García Márquez. Then Toni Morrison. Then a slim volume of Mary Oliver poems.

On the fifth day, a new barista mixes up your orders. You get a black coffee. The stranger in the corner gets an oat flat white.

They bring it to your table with a crooked grin. "I think this might be yours. Though the black coffee doesn't suit you — you look like someone who likes things a little sweet."

You blink. "You noticed my order?"

"I notice you every morning," they say simply. Like it's obvious. Like it isn't the most devastating sentence you've ever heard.

Chapter 3
Morning Rituals 🌤️

The Things Jordan Noticed

Jordan sits. Wraps both hands around the cup — your cup — and thinks for a moment.

"You always read for ten minutes before you open your laptop. You tap your pen three times before you start writing. You look at the rain like you're having a conversation with it."

A beat. "I'm Jordan, by the way."

You tell them your name. It sounds different when they say it back — like it fits better somehow.

You learn that Jordan is an architect who moved here from elsewhere, knows nobody yet, and has been coming to this café hoping to slowly become a regular. "I thought if I showed up enough, the city would start to feel like mine."

"And does it?" you ask.

Jordan looks at you steadily. "It's starting to."

Chapter 3
The Slip 😬

What You Said By Accident

Your brain short-circuits. What comes out of your mouth is:

"I noticed you too. You read books in the order of how sad they'll make you. García Márquez first, then Morrison, then the Mary Oliver because you need to end on something that still believes in the world."

Silence.

Jordan stares at you. You consider leaving the country.

Then they laugh — a real laugh, startled and delighted — and say: "That is the most accurate thing anyone has ever said about me."

They pull out the chair across from you and sit down without being invited. They order two more coffees. And you talk for two hours about grief and beauty and why the world needs people who notice things.

Chapter 2
Common Ground 🎸

The Band That Started Everything

You approach their table with a confidence that surprises you. "Is that a Phoebe Bridgers shirt? I saw her in Bristol last summer — third row."

The stranger looks up and their whole face opens like a window.

"You were at Bristol? I was front row, standing. I cried during 'Moon Song' and I'm not even slightly embarrassed about it."

You laugh. They laugh. The café feels warmer.

Their name is Jordan. They play guitar badly and listen to music obsessively and believe, truly believe, that certain songs contain a kind of portable home — you carry them with you and they make wherever you are feel liveable.

You talk for an hour. They show you a playlist on their phone called "songs for rainy tuesdays" and your heart does something it hasn't done in a long time.

Then they ask: "Do you want to go to a show? Friday. Small venue. This band you've probably never heard of, but I think you'll love them."

Chapter 3
Friday Night 🎶

Front Row

The venue is a basement with exposed brick and string lights and exactly forty-seven people who all look like they know something the rest of the city doesn't.

Jordan finds you in the crowd — "You came!" — like they weren't sure you would, and something about that uncertainty makes you want to prove every doubt wrong for the rest of your life.

The band is extraordinary. At the bridge of the third song, Jordan turns to you in the dark and says something but you can't hear it over the music. So they lean close — close enough that you can feel the warmth — and say it again into your ear:

"I'm really glad you talked to me in that coffee shop."

The song swells. The lights blur. You are completely and helplessly in trouble.

Chapter 3
The Detour 🔄

The Plans You Break

You hesitate. Jordan watches you. "No pressure," they say, and mean it.

You cancel your plans. You text your friend: Sorry, something came up. Which is technically true.

But on Friday evening, stuck in a delayed tube, you're going to be late. You text Jordan: Running behind, go in without me.

The reply: "I'm outside. I can wait."

And they do. In the November cold, hands in pockets, breath making little clouds. When you finally arrive, breathless and apologising, Jordan just smiles and says: "Told you I could wait."

Inside, the band plays a song you'll both claim as yours forever.

Chapter 4
The Almost 📲

Unknown Number

Your phone buzzes. Unknown number.

"Hi. It's Jordan. I'm so sorry — my sister is worse than we thought. I'm at the hospital. I can't make it. I feel awful."

You exhale. Then: "Don't feel awful. Be with your sister. I'll be here."

Three weeks pass. Jordan's sister recovers slowly. You text through all of it — small things, funny things, a photo of the café table you keep visiting. Jordan sends back photos from a hospital room gradually filling with plants and get-well cards.

One grey Wednesday, the café bell rings. Jordan walks in looking tired and bright-eyed and carrying something they've apparently been holding onto: a copy of One Hundred Years of Solitude, with a note tucked inside the front cover.

The note says: "For someone who keeps the table warm. Thank you."

Epilogue
✦ The Happily Ever After 💍

Every Tuesday After

Here is what nobody tells you about falling in love with someone who reads: they will leave books open on your pillow. They will read you sentences in the dark that make you understand things you couldn't put words to before.

Jordan rearranges your bookshelf by emotional weight, not alphabetically, and you let them because they are almost always right about these things.

You still go to Parchment & Brew every Tuesday morning. The barista knows your order now — both of them, yours and Jordan's — and has them ready before you reach the counter.

Sometimes you look at the corner table and think about the rainy Tuesday when everything turned on a single glance, and how terrifyingly close you came to looking away.

You didn't. That's the whole story.

That's everything.

Epilogue
✦ The Beautiful Detour 🌷

Worth the Wait

Some love stories are not late. They are simply — honestly — taking the scenic route.

Jordan's sister recovers. Jordan calls the city home, which they say took longer than expected but faster than they'd thought possible. The café is half the reason. You are the other half.

You read One Hundred Years of Solitude together — aloud, in installments, on Sunday mornings. When you get to the part where Úrsula finally finishes the house, Jordan marks the page and looks at you with something quiet and enormous in their eyes.

"She always finishes," you say.

"She does," says Jordan. "That's why I love her."

Later, you're not sure which one of you they were talking about.

Maybe that's the point.
